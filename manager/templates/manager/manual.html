Manual

Introduction

Circuitrix is a game where you get to lead a virtual racing team to glory, in competition with other participants from all over the world. Circuitrix is a simple game, it's easy to grasp the basics. You can play Circuitrix even if you just log in once or twice a week. The challenge is not to beat the game, the challenge lies within the competition with the other participants. While competing, many participants become friends, making Circuitrix also a social game.

Your overall tasks

You perform the duties of both manager and team owner. You plan tactics and strategy, manage your research and development team and select which drivers should race. You buy and sell drivers, invest in team facilities and much more. To be successful a good piece of advice is to form a good strategy and a long term plan. Success and glory will not come for free, after all.

Your new team

When you take charge of your team it has just got a vacant slot in any of the two lowest divisions in the Circuitrix series system. To start your climb upwards you got two drivers (might be talented or not), a car and some money to your aid. Now it's time for you to show your magic.

Our best piece of advice

The best piece of advice we can give you is to read the manual. You don't need to read everything at once, just reading the first chapters to start with will give you a good clue how to play the game.

To give everyone else the best Circuitrix experience there are some very simple rules of conduct to follow which you can find in our house rules.

The Car

The car is the most important factor in achieving success - it doesn't matter how skilled your drivers or how clever your tactics, if your car isn't good enough you're not going to win any races.

To evaluate a car's performance, it is important to understand the significance of its various components. The following aspects of the car directly impact its capabilities on the track:

Components
  • Engine is related to the top speed of the car.
  • Gearbox relates to the acceleration of the car. It is important for the tracks involving multiple corners.
  • Brakes are related to how fast the car can decelerate, thus high brake performance relates to longer high speed retained before the brakes have to engage to prepare for the corner.
  • Front Wing is used for slow corners. The more front wing you have the faster your car will go around slow corners.
  • Suspension relates to the maximum speed of the car going around the corner.
  • Rear Wing is used for fast corners. The more rear wing you have the faster you can go around fast corners.
How good is your car?

In the real world, when you lay eyes on a Formula car, you might describe it as a “fairly decent car” or a “truly exceptional car”. In Circuitrix, we assess the quality of a car based on the ratings of its components. Every part of the car is graded on a scale from 1 to 20: the higher the number, the more advanced the car is in that specific component. Additionally, we provide an overall rating, measured on a scale from 1 to 100, to offer a comprehensive evaluation of the car's performance.

Drivers

The driver is a paramount factor in attaining victory. Regardless of the quality of your car or the cunningness of your strategies, if your driver lacks the necessary skills, you won't be claiming any race victories.

Each driver has 5 basic skills, while there are also some additional factors affecting their performance in different situations. Let's have a rundown on the various skills first:

Skills
  • Racecraft: The ability to overtake, defend and adjust to changing conditions.
  • Pace: The ability to complete a lap as quickly as possible.
  • Focus: Drivers with higher focus are smooth, accurate and have more control.
  • Car Management: The ability to manage tires, fuel and ERS more efficiently.
  • Feedback: The outcome of your car setup depends on how well a driver can communicate to his engineer during practice, development and even during the race.

Racetracks

To assess a car's performance on a track, it's essential to consider the track's characteristics, including its length, style, and specific information. The following aspects of the track are crucial in determining how well the car will perform:

  • Track length: The length of the track affects various performance factors. Longer tracks provide more opportunities for high-speed sections, where a car with a powerful engine can excel. On the other hand, shorter tracks may have more tight corners, emphasizing the importance of acceleration and handling..
  • Straights: Tracks with longer straight sections favor cars with better engine performance. A higher percentage of the track dedicated to straights benefits cars that can achieve higher top speeds. Additionally, good braking capabilities are crucial for maximizing performance at the end of straights when preparing for corners.
  • Slow corners: Tracks with a significant number of slow corners demand cars with excellent front wing performance. A higher front wing level allows the car to generate more downforce, enabling faster cornering speeds in slow corners. Additionally, a well-tuned gearbox is vital for efficient acceleration out of these corners.
  • Fast corners: Tracks featuring fast corners require cars with an above average rear wing level. More rear wing generates increased downforce, enabling better stability and higher speeds through fast corners. Suspension characteristics also play a significant role in maximizing the car's speed and control around these corners.
How do we calculate how fast a car is on a track?

We factor in the specific characteristics of the track where the race takes place. These factors, like the track's straight sections, slow corners, and fast corners, affect how we weigh each component's importance for that particular track. Suffice it to say that a well-balanced car with high-rated components tailored to the track's features can lead to impressive speeds and, hopefully, victory on the track.

Championship system

A Circuitrix season is 12 weeks long: 10 championship rounds (10 teams in each series who race on 10 different tracks), one week for qualifiers and one week with no championship activity.

Series tree

In the world of Circuitrix, the series tree is a hierarchical structure that categorizes teams into championships, and these championships are further organized into divisions. Each championship has 10 teams, and the highest division exclusively features a single championship. Let's take a closer look at how the series system is structured:
1 Division I (Circuitrix series)
2 Division II (II.1, II.2)
4 Division III (III.1, III.2, III.3, III.4)
8 Division IV (IV.1, IV.2, etc. to IV.8)
16 Division V (V.1, V.2, etc. to V.16)
32 Division VI (VI.1, VI.2, etc. to VI.32)
64 Division VII (VII.1, VII.2, etc. to VII.64)

Promotion and relegation

If you win your championship, you earn a direct promotion. However, if you find yourself in the 9th or 10th position, you will face relegation to the division below, with the exception of the lowest division.
